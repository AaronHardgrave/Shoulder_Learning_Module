1. Radial Nerve Injury and Wrist Drop

Anatomy

The radial nerve (C5–T1) arises from the posterior cord and courses through the triangular interval with the profunda brachii artery. It travels along the radial (spiral) groove on the posterior humeral shaft between the lateral and medial heads of the triceps before piercing the lateral intermuscular septum.

Mechanism

Humeral shaft fractures at or near the radial groove place the nerve at risk. Also the nerve injured in "Saturday night palsy" from prolonged compression of the arm against a hard surface.

Presentation

Loss of wrist and finger extension (wrist drop). Significantly weakened grip because wrist extensors normally stabilize the wrist during power grip. Sensory loss over the posterior arm, posterior forearm, and dorsal hand.

Key concept

The radial nerve supplies all extensors. Its position in the radial groove makes it vulnerable to humeral shaft fractures. Knowing where the nerve is tells you what will be lost.

2. Shoulder Separation (AC Joint Injury)

Anatomy

The AC joint is stabilized by the AC ligament (direct stabilization) and the coracoclavicular ligaments — the conoid and trapezoid — which anchor the clavicle to the coracoid process and resist superior displacement.

Mechanism

Direct blow or fall onto the point of the shoulder, driving the scapula inferiorly while the clavicle remains in place. Common in contact sports and cycling accidents.

Grading
Grade I: AC ligament sprain. Stretched but intact. No visible deformity. Normal radiographs.
Grade II: AC ligament torn, CC ligaments sprained but intact. Slight clavicular elevation.
Grade III: Both AC and CC ligaments torn. Clavicle displaces superiorly — visible "step-off" deformity.
Key concept

The grading follows the anatomy in sequence. The severity reflects how many stabilizing structures have failed.

3. SLAP Tear (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior)

Anatomy

The glenoid labrum deepens the shallow glenoid fossa. The long head of the biceps brachii originates from the supraglenoid tubercle and attaches to the superior labrum — this is the site of a SLAP tear.

Mechanism

Repetitive overhead motions (throwing, swimming), fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH), or sudden traction. The biceps tendon transmits force to the labral attachment, and excessive loading detaches the superior labrum.

Presentation

Deep shoulder pain with overhead activity. Catching or locking sensation. Pain with biceps-loading maneuvers (Speed's test, O'Brien's test). Confirmed with MR arthrography or arthroscopy.

Key concept

Demonstrates the functional link between the biceps tendon and the labrum. The glenohumeral joint relies on soft tissue for stability because the bony socket is shallow.

4. Clavicle Fracture

Anatomy

The clavicle is the only bony connection between the upper extremity and the axial skeleton. It is subcutaneous along its entire length. The middle third is the thinnest part and the junction between the two curvatures.

Mechanism

The most common fracture in childhood (~10% of all fractures). Most occur in the middle third. Caused by a fall on an outstretched hand or direct blow to the shoulder.

Presentation

Pain and visible deformity over the fracture site. The lateral fragment is displaced inferiorly by the weight of the limb; the medial fragment may be pulled superiorly by the sternocleidomastoid.

Key concept

The clavicle's subcutaneous position provides no muscular padding, and the curvature transition at the middle third creates a mechanical weak point.

Question 1
A patient presents unable to abduct her arm from 0 to 15 degrees after a fall. Which muscle is most likely injured?
  • A. Deltoid (acromial head)
  • B. Infraspinatus
  • C. Supraspinatus
  • D. Trapezius (upper fibers)
The supraspinatus initiates abduction for approximately the first 15 degrees. After that, the acromial head of the deltoid takes over as the primary abductor up to about 120 degrees. If the patient cannot start the movement at all, the supraspinatus is the likely culprit.
Question 2
A construction worker falls from a ladder and fractures his humeral shaft at the level of the radial groove. On examination, he cannot extend his wrist. Which nerve is injured, and from which cord does it originate?
  • A. Radial nerve; posterior cord
  • B. Musculocutaneous nerve; lateral cord
  • C. Ulnar nerve; medial cord
  • D. Axillary nerve; posterior cord
The radial nerve travels through the radial (spiral) groove on the posterior humerus and is the most commonly injured nerve in humeral shaft fractures. It arises from the posterior cord of the brachial plexus. Loss of wrist extension (wrist drop) is the hallmark presentation because the radial nerve supplies all extensor muscles of the forearm.
Question 3
The musculocutaneous nerve is a defining feature of the anterior compartment of the arm. Which of the following is true about this nerve?
  • A. It arises from the medial cord of the brachial plexus
  • B. It passes through the triceps brachii
  • C. It pierces the coracobrachialis muscle
  • D. It terminates as the medial cutaneous nerve of the forearm
The musculocutaneous nerve arises from the lateral cord (C5, C6, C7) and pierces the coracobrachialis muscle — a useful landmark. It innervates all three anterior compartment muscles (biceps brachii, brachialis, coracobrachialis) and terminates as the lateral cutaneous nerve of the forearm.
Question 4
A 22-year-old baseball pitcher presents with deep shoulder pain and a catching sensation during throwing. Imaging reveals damage to the superior glenoid labrum at the attachment of the long head of the biceps tendon. What is this injury called?
  • A. Rotator cuff tear
  • B. Hill-Sachs lesion
  • C. Bankart lesion
  • D. SLAP tear
A SLAP (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior) tear occurs at the attachment point of the long head of the biceps tendon to the superior labrum. Repetitive overhead motions, like pitching, transmit force through the biceps tendon to the labral anchor. A Bankart lesion involves the inferior labrum (typically from anterior dislocation), and a Hill-Sachs lesion is a compression fracture of the humeral head.
Question 5
A football player takes a direct hit to the point of his shoulder. Radiographs show superior displacement of the distal clavicle with complete disruption of both the AC and CC ligaments. What grade of shoulder separation is this?
  • A. Grade I
  • B. Grade II
  • C. Grade III
  • D. Grade IV
Grade III involves complete tears of both the AC ligament and the CC ligaments (conoid and trapezoid). Because both sets of ligaments have failed, the clavicle is no longer anchored to the scapula and displaces superiorly, producing the visible "step-off" deformity. Grade I is an AC sprain only; Grade II is an AC tear with CC sprain.
Question 6
A patient has weakness in lateral rotation at the shoulder after injuring the nerve that passes through the quadrangular space. Which muscles are affected?
  • A. Supraspinatus and infraspinatus
  • B. Deltoid and teres minor
  • C. Subscapularis and teres major
  • D. Infraspinatus and teres minor
The axillary nerve (C5, C6) passes through the quadrangular space along with the posterior circumflex humeral artery. It innervates the deltoid and teres minor. The teres minor is a lateral rotator, and the deltoid (posterior head) also assists in lateral rotation. The suprascapular nerve, not the axillary, innervates the supraspinatus and infraspinatus.
Question 7
You observe a muscle with fibers running from the medial 2/3 of the subscapular fossa to the lesser tubercle of the humerus. Based on its fiber orientation and attachment points, what is its primary action?
  • A. Lateral rotation of the arm
  • B. Abduction of the arm
  • C. Medial rotation of the arm
  • D. Extension of the arm
This describes the subscapularis. Its fibers run from the anterior surface of the scapula (subscapular fossa) to the lesser tubercle, which sits on the anterior humerus. A muscle pulling from a posterior origin (back of scapula) to an anterior insertion would rotate the humerus medially. This is the principle of predicting function from fiber orientation, origin, and insertion.
Question 8
During a surgical procedure in the posterior triangle of the neck, the long thoracic nerve is accidentally severed. What clinical finding would you expect?
  • A. Winged scapula — medial border protrudes posteriorly
  • B. Shoulder drop — inability to shrug
  • C. Wrist drop — inability to extend the wrist
  • D. Loss of arm abduction
The long thoracic nerve (C5, C6, C7) innervates the serratus anterior, which holds the scapula against the thoracic wall and protracts it. When paralyzed, the medial border of the scapula protrudes posteriorly (winged scapula), especially when the patient pushes against a wall. The serratus anterior also assists in abduction by rotating the glenoid superiorly, so overhead reaching would also be compromised.
Question 9
The deltoid muscle has three distinct heads. A patient can flex and medially rotate the arm but has difficulty with pure abduction in the 15–120 degree range. Which head is most likely affected?
  • A. Clavicular head
  • B. Spine head
  • C. All three heads equally
  • D. Acromial head
The acromial (middle) head of the deltoid is the primary abductor from approximately 15 to 120 degrees. The clavicular (anterior) head performs flexion and medial rotation (both preserved here), and the spine (posterior) head performs extension and lateral rotation. The fiber orientation of the acromial head runs directly laterally from the acromion to the deltoid tuberosity, making it mechanically ideal for abduction.
Question 10
A 68-year-old woman falls on an outstretched hand and fractures the surgical neck of the humerus. In addition to nerve damage, which artery is most at risk due to its relationship with the surgical neck?
  • A. Thoracoacromial artery
  • B. Suprascapular artery
  • C. Profunda brachii artery
  • D. Posterior circumflex humeral artery
The posterior circumflex humeral artery passes through the quadrangular space along with the axillary nerve and wraps around the surgical neck of the humerus. This intimate relationship with the bone makes it vulnerable to fractures at this site. The profunda brachii artery is at risk with humeral shaft fractures at the radial groove, not the surgical neck. Compare this to Question 2: different fracture levels endanger different neurovascular structures.
