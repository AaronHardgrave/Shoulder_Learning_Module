1. Radial Nerve Injury and Wrist Drop

Anatomy

The radial nerve (C5–T1) arises from the posterior cord and courses through the triangular interval with the profunda brachii artery. It travels along the radial (spiral) groove on the posterior humeral shaft between the lateral and medial heads of the triceps before piercing the lateral intermuscular septum.

Mechanism

Humeral shaft fractures at or near the radial groove place the nerve at risk. Also injured in "Saturday night palsy" from prolonged arm compression.

Presentation

Loss of wrist and finger extension (wrist drop). Weakened grip. Sensory loss over posterior arm, posterior forearm, and dorsal hand.

Key concept

The radial nerve supplies all extensors. Its position in the radial groove makes it vulnerable. Knowing where the nerve is tells you what will be lost.

2. Shoulder Separation (AC Joint Injury)

Anatomy

The AC joint is stabilized by the AC ligament (direct) and the coracoclavicular ligaments — conoid and trapezoid — which anchor clavicle to coracoid and resist superior displacement.

Mechanism

Direct blow or fall onto the point of the shoulder. Common in contact sports and cycling.

Grading
Grade I: AC ligament sprain. Stretched but intact. No deformity.
Grade II: AC ligament torn, CC ligaments sprained but intact. Slight clavicular elevation.
Grade III: Both AC and CC ligaments torn. Visible "step-off" deformity.
Key concept

Grading follows the anatomy in sequence. Severity reflects how many stabilizers have failed.

3. SLAP Tear (Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior)

Anatomy

The glenoid labrum deepens the shallow glenoid fossa. The long head of biceps brachii attaches to the superior labrum — this is the SLAP tear site.

Mechanism

Repetitive overhead motions, FOOSH, or sudden traction. Biceps tendon transmits force to labral attachment.

Presentation

Deep shoulder pain with overhead activity. Catching sensation. Pain with Speed's and O'Brien's tests.

Key concept

Demonstrates the biceps–labrum functional link. The glenohumeral joint relies on soft tissue because the socket is shallow.

4. Clavicle Fracture

Anatomy

The clavicle is the only bony connection between upper extremity and axial skeleton. Subcutaneous along its length. Middle third is thinnest.

Mechanism

Most common fracture of childhood (~10% of all fractures). Usually middle third. FOOSH or direct blow.

Presentation

Pain and deformity. Lateral fragment displaced inferiorly; medial fragment may be pulled superiorly by SCM.

Key concept

Subcutaneous position = no muscular padding. Curvature transition at middle third = mechanical weak point.
